This course will discuss broad topics in Network Security. Topics for Fall 2025 is centered around Internet Security Protocols and the effect of evolving architectures on such protocols. Cryptographic Network Security Protocols can be broadly classified into data-plane protocols (like TLS, QUIC, IPSec, WireGuard, 802.1x etc.) and control-plane protocols like DNSSec and BGPSec. However,  irrespective of the plane any security protocol can be seen as consisting of 2 fundamental components - "Source of Truth" Functions FS(), and "Verification Functions" FV(). The former is typically independent of the specific protocol. The latter (FV()) is protocol dependent.  Source-of-truth functions serve to establish the origin of a message - which can be a control message or a data packet. Such functions typically rest on some hierarchical structure.  Once the source of truth has been established, protocol dependent verification functions determine if the data/control message satisfies some prerequisite properties (which are highly protocol specific).  The goals of this course are 2-fold:
  1. To develop a unified perspective of the 2 fundamental components in various data-plane and control-plane Network Security protocols, understand the components / parties involved in executing functions  FS() and FV(), and practical operational requirements to ensure that the components perform as intended
  2. Keeping up with advances in Network infrastructure, how changes to infrastructure can aid or hinder  the problem of securing networks.
The course will begin with a review of Symmetric and Asymmetric Cryptography. This will be followed by introductory lectures to set the stage for student presentations to follow. The first few  presentations will center around QUIC and WireGuard, and comparisons with the protocols that they aim to replace, viz., TLS and IPSec (respectively).   This will be followed by discussions and student presentations  on various topics that can include (for example)
  1. evolving architectural models for the Internet,
  2. changing dynamics of Internet traffic (and, consequently, congestion control),
  3. evolving paradigms in bandwidth-latency tradeoffs, 
  4. implications of Net Neutrality.
  5. implications of generative AI
All discussions should ultimately strive to address the question of security challenges / benefits.

ADD-DROP POLICY

  1. Add/drop without penalty

    A student has through the fifth class day into the semester to add a course and through the tenth class day to drop a course without being assessed a fee or academic penalty.

  2. Drop after the tenth class day through the 30th class day into the semester

    A student who elects to drop a course during this period must receive the approval of his/her adviser, will be assigned a W on his/her academic record, and be assessed a fee. The adviser who permits the drop will specify its effective date.

  3. Drop after the 30th class day into the semester

    A student cannot drop courses after this period except in documented cases of serious illness, extreme hardship, or failure of the instructor to provide significant assessment of his/her performance. A request to drop a course during this period must be approved by the student's adviser and academic dean. The dean who permits the drop will specify its effective date. A student receiving permission to drop will receive a W on his/her academic record and be assessed a fee.
